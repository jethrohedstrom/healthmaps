## What they do

Peer support workers draw on their **own lived experience** of mental health challenges and recovery to support others going through similar things. They don't diagnose or treat — they offer understanding, hope, and practical help navigating the system from someone who's been there.

## When to see a peer support worker

- You want support from someone who genuinely understands what it's like
- You're navigating the mental health system and want a guide who's done it
- You want connection and encouragement alongside (not instead of) clinical care
- You're part of a community program, public mental health service, or NDIS plan that offers peer work

## Medicare access

**No Medicare rebate.** Peer support is usually **free**, accessed through public health services, community organisations, or NDIS plans rather than booked privately.

## Key differences

- Support comes from lived experience, not clinical training
- Qualifications exist (like a Cert IV in Mental Health Peer Work) but none are legally required
- Complements clinical care; doesn't replace diagnosis or treatment
